Strategic partnerships to expand Airbnb in Georgia

Over the past year, Georgia has become one of the emerging hot spots for travellers worldwide. With a national population of 4.49 million, foreigners are drawn to Georgia due to its endless ranges of the Caucasus mountains and proximity to the Black Sea. Some of the most popular Georgian destinations booked through Airbnb include Tbilisi, Bakuriani, Akhaltsikhe, and Kutaisi.

In 2014, travel and tourism directly contributed 1.73 million GEL (5.9% total) to the country’s GDP. That year, the number of international tourists in Georgia totalled 4.19 million within just the first three quarters. In 2015, that number rose 4.2% higher, and is predicted to rise 6.6% every year until it accounts for 6.9% of the total GDP by 2025.

As an emerging tourist destination, it is important for us at Airbnb to help steer Georgia’s sharing economy policies, before they are established without the input of those at its core. Currently, the Georgian government has not been extremely vocal about Airbnb and its market presence.

Due to the combined factors of Airbnb market penetration and inaction of the government, this is an opportunity we must grasp in a timely way. Here are three high-level solutions based on my research.

  • Establish ties with those already friendly with government. The Georgian government currently has a very supportive attitude towards the hotel and tourism sector. They have shown this support through increased funding, beginning with 126 million GEL per annum in 2015 and going up to 134 million GEL per annum in 2018. This capital is to be allocated towards projects in infrastructure, cultural heritage protection, and more. Organisations and events such as the Euro 2016 Qualifying Matches and European Youth Athlethics Championships who already received some of this government support can be partnered with as a familiar and affordable accommodation solution.
  • Mobilize the people. Due to the country’s difficult past with often corrupt or communist governments, Georgian citizens have developed a sense of scepticism towards their government and their ability to make good change happen. As a result, there is a very strong friends and family culture in Georgia where most people rely on their loved ones to get things done. As a company and partner who can help Georgians earn a little extra side income and experience seamless cultural exchange with travellers, Airbnb should go straight to the people and leverage their tightly-knit networks to send a louder message of support for the sharing economy.

  • Partner with government. The Georgian economy is heavily labour and service concentrated. Nationally, tourism has been identified as one of the highest (third out of 12) priorities in state budgets and programs. A promise of helping increase tourism revenue by a set amount is a proposal that governments would be open to working with as this feeds into their GDP targets. Similar to past partnerships such as the one with the Korean government on rural economy, Airbnb can be the facilitator between local and state government to bring tourists to targeted areas of the country.
