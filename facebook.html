Engaging UK Politicians in the EU Referendum
Leah BaeMonday, 16 May 2016
Hi Richard thanks for reading. Im Leah and this is my application to the EMEA Politics and Government Outreach Manager role based in London. Akin to Facebooks mantra, Id rather show you my credentials than talk about them. Below, Ive compiled a proposal to get one of the UKs leading politicians on board with Facebook.
The EU referendum is coming up on June 23rd. This election is the most important poll in a generation, and the vote will be a close one with serious consequences attached. As part of this historic vote, leaders from both Leave and Remain sides will be campaigning to shape the debate. This election is not the digital election; both campaigns already understand well the profound impacts of social media and use it widely, with both Vote Leave and StrongerIn concentrating on Facebook as their platform of choice. Rather, this election will be the conversational election, and the effectiveness of online debate will be informed by the quality of creative and key players that get involved.
Its particularly important that the key players are able to influence this conversation. One of the biggest influencers in this referendum is Home Secretary Theresa May.
There are three main reasons that make the Home Secretary the ideal politician for Facebooks Politics & Government team to engage in the EU referendum right now:
  1. The major opportunity with the Home Secretary is that she has yet to develop herself a presence on any social media platform -- not even Twitter.
  2. The Home Secretary has been facing huge backlash over her recent decisions, such as her calls to withdraw from the European human rights convention, governments response to the refugee crisis, and the investigatory powers bill. Given these attacks on multiple fronts, it would be most beneficial if the Home Secretary was able to take her image into her own hands. At the same time, the Home Secretary is widely touted to be next in line for Leader of the Conservative Party, and has recently even hired ex-Sky News deputy political editor Joey Jones to be her new spokesperson and help manage her reputation in the lead up to it.
  3. The British public view the Home Secretary as a very serious and critical figure. Despite publicly announcing her support to remain in the EU, many of her past comments have reflected those of a Leave supporter. This means that for the Remain side, Theresa May is the best politician to influence the last phase of persuasion in the campaign. Having a powerful figure with the Home Secretary file who has a track record of being tough on crime and immigration will be a massive asset to persuade those teetering undecided in the referendum. The Home Secretary is the key to bringing the eurosceptics to pledge yes to Remain. In order for her to campaign effectively, she needs an effective platform to do so.
Home Secretary May is an outlier among her peers, as most prominent politicians both in the UK and elsewhere have active presence on social media. There are a number of possible reasons why she has thus far refrained from establishing social media accounts:
  1. Despite her tech-heavy portfolio, she may be that she is not as comfortable with tech as she appears to be. In my past work, I have noticed that while MPs understand the prevalence and importance of digital, often they are unable to explain the technicalities of the digital topics at hand. Although she is the instigator of policies pertaining to cyber security and internet technologies, the Home Secretary has hardly expressed interest in participating in digital herself. This is evident in her committee hearings where she is asked to explain parts of her investigatory powers bill, including end-to-end encryption.
  2. Given her current position as Home Secretary, she may be especially concerned about technology and security and therefore even less willing to engage with social media. If this is her chief concern, encouraging immediate on-boarding and active use of Facebook may be difficult. However, given her political ambitions and likely future within the party, a relationship with the Home Secretary is nonetheless an important step we must plan for looking forward.
  3. She may also be averse to technology, and controlling of the people/activities around her. In this case, a better engagement strategy may be to build a relationship with her staff first than go to her directly.
If successful in reaching the Home Secretary, I would advise and assist her in implementing the following initiatives:
  1. Leverage Facebook as a platform to reach out to voters across the country who shes never been able to reach out to before. We can build upon her existing voter contact base to identify additional similar voters by using our Custom Audiences and Lookalike Audiences tools.
  2. In the lead up to the SNPs most successful election run in UK history, Nicola Sturgeon turned to our Q&A tool to activate voters from all parts of the country. The constant social interaction from the party was one of the large contributors to their success. While face-to-face outreach should never be neglected, consistent GOTV research shows that if people are more familiar with a name/figure, they are more likely to cast a ballot for them. For this, retargeting via Facebook ads and Q&A interactions will help.
  3. It is clear from past efforts that the Conservative demographic is both targetable and receptive on Facebook. Targeting digitally is more productive and precise way to reach voters. To both strengthen and build on supporters, use clear calls-to-action to obtain voter emails to activate support and collect pledges.
Can I help you reach out to Theresa May and shape your engagement efforts? Email me@leahebae.com.
